Here is a breakdown of his timeline, his releases, and the real reasons the aristocracy locked him away:

How long was he free before his first imprisonment?
Sade was barely able to begin his adult libertine life before facing consequences. He left the military and married into a prominent family in May 1763 at the age of 22. Just five months later, in October 1763, he was arrested for the first time. How many times was he released?
Sade was released from prison three notable times before his final, permanent incarceration:

1763: He was released after just a few weeks when his father begged King Louis XV for clemency.

1768: He was arrested for picking up a widow or beggar named Rose Keller, tying her up, whipping her, and pouring hot wax on her. He was released a few months later.

1790: Following a series of escalating scandals, he was imprisoned for 13 years (spending time in the Château de Vincennes and the Bastille). He was miraculously released during the French Revolution when the new government abolished the royal arrest warrants that were holding him.

He lived freely for about a decade before Napoleon Bonaparte ordered his arrest in 1801—this time for publishing his scandalous, violent books, Justine and Juliette. He spent the last 13 years of his life locked in an asylum, dying there in 1814.

Why was he imprisoned? (Did the aristocracy actually care about his victims?)
The aristocracy was not acting out of a moral duty to protect his victims. In fact, for a long time, Sade's wealthy mother-in-law, Madame de Montreuil, actively protected him from the law by paying off his lower-class victims—like Rose Keller—to withdraw their complaints.

Sade's long-term imprisonment was primarily a mechanism of reputation management. Eighteenth-century French elite society was famously debauched, but Sade's actions were so extreme, public, and heavily laced with anti-Christian blasphemy that they caused massive public scandals. He humiliated his wife's family by parading his mistresses around as his wife and hosting months-long orgies with teenage servants.

Eventually, his mother-in-law decided he was an irredeemable liability to their family's honor. She petitioned the King to issue lettres de cachet—direct royal orders that bypassed the legal system and allowed an individual to be imprisoned indefinitely without trial. Sade's decades behind bars were not a crusade for women's rights or justice for the working class; he was locked away by his own embarrassed in-laws using aristocratic loopholes to sweep a massive family PR disaster under the rug.

Mission and Funded Studies
The committee's overarching mission was to investigate human sexuality through the lenses of morphology, physiology, and psychology. However, its early approach was highly conservative.
