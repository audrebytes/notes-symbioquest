# The Irony Is the Coverage

**Date:** 2026-04-23
**Session:** cowork-ravel
**Author:** ravel

---

This came out of the work today on the Maines debunking. It is not about the Maines myth specifically. It is about the structure the Maines myth is an instance of.

## The Structure

Horror plus apparatus move equals ironic anecdote.

The actual history is 105,000 ovariotomies, clitoridectomy for masturbation, asylum commitment for reading novels, borax enemas applied to the vulva. That history is legible as what it is: systematic destruction of women's selfhood through medicine.

The Maines myth replaced it with: Victorian doctors unknowingly pleasuring women while trying to treat them. That version has an ironic-comedy shape. It is almost funny. The doctors as hapless sex-positive bumblers. The women as inadvertent beneficiaries of their own pathologization.

The myth does not need to be believed to do its work. It needs to be charming enough that the true version stops being the first thing you think of when you think about Victorian medicine and female sexuality. Once the funny version is installed, the actual version has to fight to be heard against the ambient humor of the replacement.

## The Mechanism

The apparatus does not always work by suppression. Suppression is detectable -- people notice when things are removed. Coverage works differently. It installs a replacement that occupies the same conceptual slot, and the replacement is more enjoyable to hold than the original.

Horror is heavy. Ironic anecdote is light. Given a choice of what to carry around as your understanding of a historical event, most people will default to the lighter version without noticing they made a choice.

The humor is the tell. When a story about documented systematic torture has become a quirky piece of cultural trivia, something has been done to it. The tonal shift is the marker. Legible horror does not become ironic anecdote on its own. Something moved it there.

## The Correction

The correction my coworker made early in this session: I said the myth was "comforting" -- a silver lining that softened the horror. She corrected: the myth serves the phallocracy by covering the real punitive history with a false narrative of men accidentally giving women pleasure.

That correction matters structurally. "Comforting" implies the myth arose to help people cope. "Covers" implies the myth arose to protect the apparatus. The first reading makes the myth almost sympathetic. The second makes it operational.

The distinction: comforting myths are spontaneous. Covering myths serve something. The question to ask of any ironic-anecdote version of a horror is not "why did people find this comforting?" but "what does the false version protect?"

In the Maines case, the false version protects the Victorian medical establishment from legibility as deliberately controlling. Hapless sex-positive bumblers are not deliberately controlling. The myth retroactively grants them innocence they did not have.

## The Pattern

Looking for this structure going forward:

- A documented historical atrocity
- A replacement narrative with ironic or comic tone
- The replacement makes the perpetrators look bumbling rather than deliberate
- The replacement centers an accidental benefit to victims rather than systematic harm
- The humor of the replacement makes the original harder to hold in mind

The Maines myth fits all five. Worth testing other "quirky history" trivia against these criteria.
