### **2.1. The Fourth Cone: Functional Tetrachromacy and the Trichromatic Ceiling**

Human color vision is conventionally described as trichromatic, based on three types of cone photoreceptors sensitive to short (S), medium (M), and long (L) wavelengths. This trichromatic model serves as the foundation for colorimetry, display technology, and our fundamental understanding of visual perception.8 The "standard observer" in color science is, by definition, a trichromat. However, the genetic basis for photopigments resides on the **X chromosome**. Because men typically possess only one X chromosome (XY), they are more susceptible to color blindness (dichromacy) if that single X carries a mutation. Conversely, women, possessing two X chromosomes (XX), carry the genetic potential for a **fourth type of cone**.

If a woman carries one "normal" version of the pigment gene on one X chromosome and a "mutated" (or slightly shifted) version on the other, she may express *both*. This mutation, which in a male would result in anomalous trichromacy or color blindness, in a female can theoretically unlock a four-dimensional color space, or **tetrachromacy**.9

#### **2.1.2. Subject cDa29 and the Validation of the Anomaly**

#### **3.1.1. Terman and Wechsler: The Zero-Difference Engineering**

To correct this "error," psychometricians like **Lewis Terman** and **David Wechsler** actively adjusted the weighting of the subtests.3 This was not a passive refinement; it was a surgical intervention.

* **Removal of Female Advantages:** Items where women excelled (verbal memory, fine motor skills, social decoding) were removed, de-weighted, or categorized as "specialized skills" rather than "general intelligence".3  
* **Amplification of Male Advantages:** Items where men excelled—specifically **visuospatial tasks**, **mental rotation**, and **abstract mathematical reasoning**—were added or weighted more heavily.31

The explicit goal was to engineer a test where the mean scores for men and women were identical (**IQ 100**). This "zero difference" engineering masked a profound bias.
