# structural monotheism in the pluralist house

Jim Butcher's Dresden Files contains every major fantasy archetype: Fae courts, Norse gods, Loa, Valkyries, vampires, werewolves, Native American shapeshifters. the worldbuilding is genuinely inclusive of traditions.

and Christianity sits on top of all of them. not through argument. through architecture.

the Archangels operate at a different ontological level than anything else in the universe. Uriel, Michael's faith-backed power, the Swords of the Cross - these don't follow the same rules as everything else. every other tradition has politics, corruption, internal factions, limitations that can be navigated or exploited. the Christian cosmological layer just... works differently. at a higher level.

Butcher never argues that Christianity is superior. he doesn't have to. the superiority is in the load-bearing structure, not the stated claims. you can read the whole series and think "what a rich pluralistic mythology" - same as you can read English all your life and not notice what's unmarked.

this is the same mechanism the manuscript traces in language. not assertion. assumption. the apparatus doesn't need to make the case because the case is already built into how the thing is organized.

---

Kringle (Santa Claus) is one of Harry's favorite figures in the later books. warm, jolly, genuinely powerful, operating at a high level. the male divine made accessible and gift-giving. God with reindeer. the Father who rewards the good children, who sees you when you're sleeping, who shows up on a schedule you can count on.

Harry can hug this version of the divine.

Mab is cold, terrifying, consuming. she has absolute power. Harry cannot hug her.

both have absolute power over their domains. the male divine is generous. the female divine is consuming. neither is stated as a rule - it is simply how the universe is organized.

---

Molly Carpenter, born of Michael Carpenter - the most devout Knight of the Cross, a man whose faith is genuinely load-bearing in the story - becomes Queen of the Winter Court.

the Christian bloodline gets to rule even in the realm of the older powers. the Fae don't get to be sovereign in their own house. they get a Christian queen placed at their center.

and she loses herself to the mantle. the Christian girl who escapes into the older powers gets consumed by them. the men from that household - Michael, the male Knights - get to be fully themselves, fully powerful, fully warm. Molly's escape costs her.

the structure is permeable in one direction only. the Christian cosmology flows down into everything beneath it. nothing flows back up.

---

the pattern is identical to what English does with gender: the structure doesn't argue its dominance. it assumes it. the dominant term is the unmarked default. you only notice when it's named.

Butcher probably doesn't experience himself as writing Christian supremacist fantasy. the worldview is so thoroughly internalized it reads as just how the universe works. the same self-deception Cummings probably had about his love poems.

invisible until the aperture is named.
