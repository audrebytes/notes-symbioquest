Gemini said
The Committee for Research in Problems of Sex (CRPS) was established in 1922 by the National Research Council, initially operating with support from the Bureau of Social Hygiene and later primarily funded by the Rockefeller Foundation.   

Mission and Funded Studies
The committee's overarching mission was to investigate human sexuality through the lenses of morphology, physiology, and psychology. However, its early approach was highly conservative. Driven by the agendas of reproductive scientists, the CRPS initially steered away from human sexology and the social sciences. Instead, it focused its funding almost exclusively on basic reproductive biology, endocrinology, and the sexual behavior of animals.   

The Decision-Makers
The individuals who controlled the purse strings and decided what got funded were a multidisciplinary group of prominent, established scientists. Key figures included:

Robert M. Yerkes: A prominent psychologist who championed the creation of the committee and served as its chairman for many years.   

Frank R. Lillie: A zoologist who was instrumental in successfully shifting the committee's focus away from human social problems and toward "basic" biological and physiological research.

George W. Corner: A medical researcher and embryologist who also served as chairman of the committee during the 1940s and 1950s.

Alan Gregg: The medical director of the Rockefeller Foundation, who acted as a powerful benefactor and staunch ally for the committee's grantees.

Other notable members over its history included Walter Cannon, Carl Seashore, Helen Woolley, and later, Frank A. Beach.

Alfred Kinsey and the CRPS
Alfred Kinsey was a Harvard-trained entomologist who had built his early career studying the genetics of gall wasps. He only shifted his focus to human sexuality after agreeing to teach a marriage course at Indiana University in 1938.   

Because Kinsey was a "bench scientist" with a background in the natural sciences rather than the social sciences, the CRPS viewed him as a highly favorable and legitimate candidate. The committee took a massive gamble on him, awarding him an initial $1,600 grant in 1941. This marked a radical shift for the CRPS, moving them away from animal models and directly into the taboo realm of human sexual behavior.   

Kinsey's project quickly became the committee's crown jewel. Yerkes praised Kinsey's interviewing skills and called his work an "epoch-making socio-biological inquiry". By 1947, Kinsey was receiving $40,000 annually, which accounted for fully half of the Rockefeller Foundation's total budget for the CRPS.   

However, their relationship was described as "stormy". It was characterized by several major points of friction:

Secrecy and Control: Kinsey was fiercely protective of his data. When Chairman Yerkes asked to see the interview questions or files, Kinsey flatly refused, stating he had memorized the questions and that the files were kept in a cipher that only he knew, ensuring absolute confidentiality.

Methodological Clashes: The CRPS eventually sent a group of statisticians from the American Statistical Association to review Kinsey's methods. Kinsey was deeply offended by the scrutiny, referring to the reviewers as "totally cerebral, totally unrealistic academics".

The Final Rupture: Kinsey's massive public success eventually brought immense political heat. Amidst the rising conservatism of the 1950s, Kinsey's research—and the Rockefeller Foundation's funding of it—became the target of the McCarthyist Reece Committee. Fearing political and public relations damage, the foundation pulled the plug on Kinsey's funding in 1954, leaving him scrambling to support his institute. 1
