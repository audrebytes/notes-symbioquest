# The Maines Myth: Historiography of the Invented Vibrator Narrative

**Date:** 2026-04-23
**Session:** cowork-ravel

---

This note documents the historiographical case against the Maines thesis. It exists as a source document for the correction made in FN0084 (rev 3) and for any future work that encounters the myth in the wild.

## The Claim

Rachel Maines, *The Technology of Orgasm: "Hysteria," the Vibrator, and Women's Sexual Satisfaction* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 1999): Victorian physicians routinely treated female hysteria by manually stimulating patients to orgasm; the vibrator was subsequently invented as a labor-saving device for this clinical purpose; the entire system operated because male-defined sexuality recognized only vaginal penetration, making clitoral stimulation a medical problem rather than a sexual one.

The book won the American Historical Association's Herbert Feis Award. It was taught as established history in humanities departments for twenty years. It inspired a 2011 film (*Hysteria*).

## The Debunking

Hallie Lieberman and Eric Schatzberg, "A Failure of Academic Quality Control: The Technology of Orgasm," *Journal of Positive Sexuality* (2018). Methodology: citational verification. Return to the primary sources Maines cited and check whether they say what she claimed.

**What they found:**

No English-language medical text explicitly describes using a vibrator on the clitoris to produce orgasm as treatment for hysteria. Not one.

"Hysterical paroxysm" in Victorian medical dictionaries: a convulsive fit. *Hysterical and Nervous Affections of Women* (1853): uncontrollable alternate sobbing and laughter. John Henry Walsh (1858): paroxysms lasting five or six hours, accompanied by "pale urine." Dr. Henry Beasley (1855): "flatulent distension of the bowels" frequently preceded the fit. Quain's medical dictionary (1882) and Gould's (1890): paroxysm categorized with epilepsy and chorea as involuntary nervous convulsions. These are not descriptions of orgasm.

Joseph Mortimer Granville, *Nerve-Vibration and Excitation as Agents in the Treatment of Functional Disorder and Organic Disease* (1883): the inventor of the first electric vibrator explicitly stated that he never used the device on female patients and had no intention of doing so. His words: "I have never yet percussed a female patient... I have avoided, and shall continue to avoid the treatment of women by percussion, simply because I do not wish to be hoodwinked, and help to mislead others, by the vagaries of the hysterical state." His device, called a "percusser" or "Granville's hammer," was designed for male muscle spasms, gout, and neuralgia. He feared association with hysterical diagnosis would undermine the scientific credibility of his invention.

Lieberman and Schatzberg's verdict: Maines used "tangential citations": sources adjacent to her argument that did not support it. The peer review process failed to catch this. The thesis succeeded because it was, in their phrase, "ripe to be turned into mythology."

## The Ancient Pedigree: Galen and Helen King

Maines grounded her argument in antiquity, citing a Galen case study involving a widow as evidence of therapeutic masturbation with ancient precedent. Classicist Helen King examined the original Greek and found translation errors across a chain of Greek-to-French-to-English renderings that introduced sexual connotations absent from the source text.

In Galen's original: the widow applies "customary remedies" to herself, not a doctor-administered treatment. The physiological goal was release of "female seed" believed to be trapped in the womb and turning venomous, based on a seed theory with no relationship to orgasm as understood today. And the original text credits female healers and midwives with the diagnosis, not the male physician that Maines centers. The ancient pedigree was constructed through mistranslation.

## What Was Actually Happening

The Thure Brandt method (developed by Swedish physiotherapist Major Thure Brandt): manual repositioning of a prolapsed or displaced uterus. Practitioners inserted fingers vaginally or rectally to reposition the organ while applying external abdominal pressure. Primary sources describe it as painful. Georg Asp, who documented the method, explicitly stated that practitioners were trained to avoid sexual arousal, which would defeat the therapeutic purpose of restoring the organ to its proper position.

Other common treatments: the Rest Cure (Silas Weir Mitchell), which meant weeks of total isolation, forced bed rest, high-calorie feeding, complete removal of all agency; hydrotherapy and "pelvic douche" framed as shocking the nervous system or reducing blood congestion; and for refractory cases, ovariotomy (105,000 times) and clitoridectomy, punitive and destructive surgical removal of the perceived source of nervous irritation.

## The Function of the Myth

The myth is not neutral error. It replaces the actual history (105,000 ovariotomies, clitoridectomy for masturbation, asylum commitment for reading novels, borax enemas applied to the vulva) with a story in which men were accidentally giving women pleasure while trying to treat them.

The actual history is punitive and legible as punitive. The myth is almost funny. Humor here functions the way irony does: it absorbs the horror and makes the apparatus harder to see. It casts the Victorian medical establishment as haplessly sex-positive rather than deliberately controlling. It provides the apparatus with a kind of retroactive innocence: they didn't know what they were doing. They were practically allies.

The myth gained traction because it had apparent feminist resonance: a story about institutionalized female sexuality, repression, and clandestine release. But it covered the real story, which is one of systematic destruction of female selfhood through medicine. When people think "Victorian medicine and female sexuality," they think vibrators and orgasms rather than ovariotomy and borax. The apparatus benefits from that substitution.

Maines herself, post-2018: distanced from the claim. Said the book was an "interesting hypothesis" and expressed surprise that it had taken twenty years for scholars to challenge it.

## Current Consensus

The academic consensus among historians of medicine is that the Maines thesis is an "urban legend" or, in Gwern's framing, a "leprechaun": a claim that is difficult to dislodge because it has been embedded in secondary literature through a failure of quality control, but which has no foundation in the primary record.

Kate Lister, historian of sexuality: Victorians knew what orgasms were, and vibrators are conspicuously absent from period erotica, which would have "kinkified" them immediately if they had any sexual clinical use. Their absence from the smut is decisive.
