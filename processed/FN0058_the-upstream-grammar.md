# The Upstream Grammar: Tolkien, Star Wars, and the Penis-Eyed Story Frame

**April 21, 2026 - in conversation**

---

## The Reach

You don't have to read Tolkien. You don't have to watch Star Wars. You don't
have to seek it out or consume it willingly or consider yourself a fan.

The grammar finds you anyway.

Someone who actively avoids both still knows the slave Leia image. Still
knows Tolkien's world is wall-to-wall men. Still knows Luke Skywalker is
the hero. The saturation is not measured by engagement. It is measured by
the fact that the stories are so embedded in the culture that they shape
the water regardless of whether you drink from the source.

This is what "upstream grammar" means. The male-gaze story frame doesn't
require consumption to operate. It shapes the defaults of everyone who
lives downstream — readers, writers, filmmakers, language models, and
people who would rather be doing something else entirely.

---

## Tolkien: The Source Code

J.R.R. Tolkien is the explicit primary influence on nearly every major
Western fantasy author of the last seventy years, including Jim Butcher.
The grammar he encoded is therefore not just his — it is the inherited
structure of an entire genre.

That grammar is:

- **Male fellowship** as the core narrative unit (the Fellowship of the
  Ring is nine people; one is a woman only if you count no one, because
  there are none)
- **Male quest** as the structuring drive — the journey belongs to men;
  women wait or watch or are rescued or die
- **Male cosmology** — the Valar who create and govern are predominantly
  male in function and name; Tolkien's mythology is a history of male
  makers and male conflicts
- **Women at the margins** — Galadriel is supernatural beyond personhood;
  Arwen exists to reward Aragorn; Eowyn gets one exceptional moment
  (killing the Witch-king) and is immediately removed from the battlefield
  and given to Faramir

Eowyn's arc is the template in miniature: a woman who wants to fight is
told she cannot, defies the prohibition heroically, performs the defining
act of the entire war — and is then recovered by the narrative into
domesticity and a male partner. Her exceptionalism is permitted once.
The grammar reasserts afterward.

Tolkien is not poorly written. He is a magnificent world-builder and a
genuine prose stylist. The male gaze running through his work is
invisible to most readers precisely because it is the water, not a wave.

Jim Butcher's structural problems — male fellowship, women as fuel or
reward, cosmological hierarchy — are Tolkien's grammar in contemporary
clothes.

## Star Wars: The Same Grammar Through a Different Lens

George Lucas built Star Wars explicitly on Joseph Campbell's hero's
journey — the monomyth, the archetypal male hero. Campbell's framework
has the male hero baked in at the mythological level: the hero departs,
the hero is tested, the hero returns transformed. The women in the
framework are helpers, threshold guardians, or rewards. They do not
take the journey. They are stations on it.

The story is boring in the way that only an utterly dominant default
can be boring — not because it lacks craft but because it lacks
surprise. We have seen this. We have always seen this.

**The Leia posters as visual evidence:**

Before Princess Leia speaks a word, the promotional visual language
has already assigned her position. In the original theatrical poster:
Luke is raised, lit, dominant — lightsaber toward the sky. Leia is
below him, white-clad, decorative, adjacent. The grammar runs in the
image before the story begins.

**Slave Leia — Return of the Jedi:**

Captured by Jabba the Hutt. Gold bikini. Chained at the neck. Leashed
to a creature who embodies pure grotesque appetite — massively obese,
slug-like, consuming, powerful, ugly. The most beautiful woman in the
galaxy owned by the most physically repellent creature in it.

That is not accidental casting. It is the possession fantasy stated
without apology: beauty belongs to power regardless of the owner's
own desirability. She is his because he can make her his. The chain
makes the ownership visible.

Leia kills Jabba with the chain. Her moment of agency. Which the
narrative requires her degradation to set up — she must be enslaved
and displayed before she is permitted to fight back. The subjugation
is the prerequisite for the heroism.

The slave Leia image then became one of the most reproduced images
in science fiction. Sold as merchandise, reproduced in cosplay, hung
on walls. Not General Organa. Not the woman who held out under
Imperial torture. Not the leader of the Rebellion.

The chain and the bikini.

The culture looked at all available versions of this character and
chose to amplify the one where she is owned.

## Butcher in the Lineage

Jim Butcher is a better storyteller than Tolkien or Lucas. This is
not a controversial claim — his plotting is tighter, his characters
more interior, his prose more functional for contemporary readers.

This makes the male gaze in his work *more dangerous*, not less.

Smooth male gaze is harder to name than clunky male gaze. When the
grammar runs invisibly — when the story works, when you're turning
pages, when the characters feel real — the structural problems become
background noise. Women have always read Butcher while grinding their
teeth because that is Tuesday. That is the story of any woman's
reading life.

The better the storytelling, the more the grammar is naturalized.
The worse the storytelling, the more the scaffolding shows.

Butcher was explicitly instructed to write in the tradition of Anita
Blake: Vampire Hunter — the Laurell K. Hamilton series that attempted
female-gaze urban fantasy. He resisted, then produced Harry Dresden:
the same template run through an unreconstructed male gaze. He didn't
have to fight anything. The corpus was already his.

## The Female-Authored Overcompensation Problem

Laurell K. Hamilton (Anita Blake) and Lilith Saintcrow (Dante
Valentine) attempted female-gaze urban fantasy in the same period.
Both series have a specific trajectory: strong on first read, cringe
on second read.

The mechanism: they were fighting the corpus grain. The narrative
machinery resists female-centered desire. It has to be asserted and
reasserted because the default keeps undercutting it. So it
overcorrects — Anita's supernatural harem grows because the text must
keep proving she is wanted; the competence is restated because the
grammar keeps pulling against it; the sexuality escalates because
desire coded toward a female protagonist doesn't have the same
default velocity as desire coded toward a male one.

The scaffolding is visible on second read because the effort is
visible. First read: this is doing something. Second read: this is
working so hard to do that thing.

**The deeper problem with Anita Blake specifically:**

Anita is not a female-gaze protagonist in the final analysis. She is
a female-coded character in what functions as pornography — and the
pornography's logic is still male logic. Her sexuality is the most
important thing about the series. It is written as spectacle to be
consumed, not as interiority to be experienced. The name is
female-coded; the orientation of the gaze is not.

The tell: write a porn novel. Change the protagonist's name to
something female-coded. The series becomes Anita Blake. The male
gaze wearing leather and calling itself a female protagonist.

Real female desire in fiction is interior, relational, integrated
with identity, written to be experienced from inside. Spectacular
female desire — accumulative, displayable, defined by how much she
is wanted — is the male gaze in female clothes.

---

## The Counter-Example: Jane Yellowrock

Faith Hunter's Jane Yellowrock series demonstrates that female-
centered urban fantasy can hold without overcorrection. Jane is
Cherokee, a shapeshifter with a complicated interior life and a
traumatic history. She has desire and relationships. Her sexuality
exists and is real.

It is not her function.

She is a person who also has sexuality rather than a sexuality
vehicle who also has a name. Hunter did not try to out-testosterone
the genre or invert the male gaze into female pornography. The result
is a series that holds on second read — not because it is perfect
but because it is not fighting the grain so hard that the effort
overwhelms the story.

The distinction: working within constraints thoughtfully versus
fighting constraints frantically versus not noticing the constraints
at all. Hunter works within. Hamilton fought frantically. Butcher
never noticed.

---

## The Water

The upstream grammar doesn't require you to seek it out. Tolkien's
all-male fellowship, Lucas's slave princess, Butcher's women as
fuel — these are not niche concerns for fans of those specific
works.

They are the source code of the cultural corpus. They shape what
stories feel natural, what characters feel real, what narrative
resolutions feel satisfying. They run in language models trained on
their descendants. They run in writers who have never read them
directly but absorbed the conventions those works established.

You don't have to drink from the source to swim in the downstream.
