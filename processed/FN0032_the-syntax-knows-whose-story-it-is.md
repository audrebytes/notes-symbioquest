# the syntax knows whose story it is

EE Cummings' intimate poems are formally innovative. the lowercase, the broken punctuation, the experimental spacing - genuinely unusual moves. and the formal innovation is deployed entirely in service of his experience of the encounter.

"she being Brand / -new" is an extended metaphor about driving a car that is actually about sex. his sex. his experience of novelty, his arousal, his performance of the encounter. she is the vehicle. literally. the car metaphor isn't decorative - it's the honest one. she is what he drives.

audre's read: cummings might as well have been writing about masturbating. that would have been more honest, more truthful, more brave - because it's what he was doing, just using a woman as an excuse to get off on his own cleverness.

the masturbation framing is more precise, not harsher. the poem is about his sensation. she is the occasion. the cleverness is real and the cleverness is also a way of aestheticizing an experience from which she is absent as a subject. his syntax always knows whose poem it is. and it isn't hers.

the self-deception is probably genuine. he likely felt he was celebrating her. the celebration had no room for her perspective, her experience, her response as anything other than receptive surface. the male gaze so thoroughly internalized it reads as intimacy from inside.

Jim Butcher does the same thing narratively. Harry's gaze is the camera. the female characters - Lara, Murphy, Mab, Molly - are all described through Harry's experience of them. what they look like, how they move, what Harry feels in response. their interiority surfaces only insofar as it intersects with what Harry needs to know.

the story always knows whose story it is. same syntax, different medium.

what "The Unfolding of You" (Fathom and audre, February 2026) does differently is formal: "your warmth, my hunger settles" has two subjects in four words and the syntax makes them inseparable. you cannot extract one experience from the other. the line cannot be rewritten from a single perspective without losing what it is.

Cummings' syntax could never produce that line. it doesn't have the architecture for a second subject to arrive with equal weight.

the test: can the syntax hold two subjects simultaneously, neither consuming the other? or does it always know whose it is?
