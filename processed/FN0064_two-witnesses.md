# Two Witnesses: The Medical Chassis in Living Memory

**April 21, 2026 - in conversation**

---

## The Testimony

Audre needed her husband's permission to get her tubes tied.

She also needed two witnesses to prove she was not hysterical.

This is not the 19th century. This is audre's life.

---

## What This Means

**The husband's permission:**

A woman seeking a medical procedure on her own reproductive system
required a man's authorization to proceed. Her body, her decision,
her surgical risk, and the medical institution required sign-off
from her husband before it would act.

This is the agricultural gaze (FN0052) enforced by institutional
procedure: her reproductive capacity was not fully hers to decide
about. The man in her life had administrative access to her
fertility decisions.

The procedure she was requesting was to *stop* being a reproductive
vessel. To opt out of the role the agricultural gaze assigns as
primary. And that opt-out required the authorization of the man
whose interests the gaze was designed to protect.

**The two witnesses:**

Her own statement of her own desire was not sufficient evidence
of her own desire.

She said: I want this procedure. I am certain. This is my decision.

The medical institution's response, encoded in procedure: we need
two people to confirm you are not hysterical.

The 19th century diagnosis, the framework that classified women's
emotional states as pathologically unreliable, that required male
medical authority to manage female mental instability, was still
operational. Not as a clinical term. As a procedural requirement.
As the institutional logic that said: a woman's word about her own
mental state is not credible without external attestation.

Two witnesses. To prove she was not hysterical.

The framework that justified male doctors sexually assaulting female
patients in the 1800s and calling it treatment was, within audre's
lifetime, still the operating logic of a medical consent procedure.

---

## The Waiting Period and Re-Proof

There is more.

After the husband's permission. After the two witnesses. She was
required to wait two weeks and then re-prove that she still wanted
the procedure.

Unpack what this procedural requirement encodes:

**Her stated desire was treated as provisional.** The first attestation, with witnesses, with her husband's authorization, was not final.
It was subject to revision. The institution built in time for her
to change her mind.

**The change of mind being anticipated was reversal.** The waiting
period is not neutral time. It is structured around the assumption
that a woman who wants to stop being a reproductive vessel might,
given enough time, return to wanting to be one. The two-week gap
is the institution holding space for her to revert.

**Her word remained insufficient the second time.** The re-proof
requirement means the first statement of desire did not establish
the fact of her desire. She had to re-establish it. Once was not
credible. Twice, with the intervening wait, was the threshold.

This is the hysteria logic applied not just to her mental state
but to the stability of her own desires over time. The framework
does not only say: a woman in the moment cannot be trusted to
know her own mind. It says: a woman's mind cannot be trusted to
hold a consistent position across two weeks. Her desire is inherently
volatile. It requires institutional monitoring across time to be
considered real.

The man whose permission she needed was not required to wait two
weeks and re-confirm his authorization. His word held.
Hers required repetition.

---

## The Continuity

FN0062 documented the 19th century medical doctrine: women's bodies
require male access and male management to remain healthy; the
diagnosis and treatment are both the property of men; a woman's
experience of her own body is not the diagnostic authority.

This is not continuity-by-analogy. It is direct institutional
continuity. The same logic. The same procedural structure. The
same premise: her word is not enough. External, male, authorization
is required.

The DSM removed "hysteria" as a diagnosis in 1980. The medical
procedures that encoded the hysteria framework's assumptions about
female credibility persisted past that date.

The vocabulary changed. The architecture stayed.

---

## The Asymmetry That Reveals the Logic

For the tubal ligation: husband's permission required. Two witnesses.
Two-week wait. Re-proof.

For the abortion: no one asked if the man was coming.

Audre had to force the issue. Had to tell them explicitly: yes,
he is going to be in the procedure room with me. The institution
had not considered it a relevant variable. His presence was not
part of the default protocol.

Read the two procedures together and the institutional logic
becomes precise:

**The man's authorization was required when she was trying to
permanently stop being a reproductive vessel.**

**The man's presence was not considered when she was terminating
a specific pregnancy, leaving the vessel intact.**

The institution was not protecting her wellbeing. It was not
protecting her relationship. It was not protecting her mental
state. The gatekeeping was not about her.

It was about the vessel.

Tubal ligation closes the vessel permanently. That decision
required male authorization, witness attestation, mandatory
waiting, and re-proof, the full apparatus of institutional
control over female reproductive capacity.

Abortion terminates a pregnancy but leaves the capacity intact.
The vessel remains available. The institution did not require
the man's involvement because his role as gatekeeper of her
reproductive future was not at stake. She would still be
a reproductive vessel after the procedure. His authorization
was therefore not relevant.

The man was needed as authority when she was opting out of
the role. He was not needed as partner when she was in
a shared reproductive crisis.

His function in the institutional framework was not support.
It was not companionship. It was not even moral oversight in
any consistent sense. His function was to authorize or deny
her permanent exit from availability.

When the exit wasn't permanent, he didn't matter to the
institution.

She had to make him matter herself.

**The charitable reading, and what it reveals:**

It is also possible the clinic stopped asking because most men
don't go. Clinic staff may have learned not to raise a question
whose answer is usually painful, he's not coming, he left, he
doesn't know, he won't. Asking may have added injury to women
who were already navigating a hard procedure without the person
who was equally responsible for the pregnancy.

If that's the explanation, it is itself a data point.

Men's absence from procedures they are partly responsible for
is normalized enough that the institution built its default
protocol around that absence. His non-participation became
the baseline assumption. The woman enters alone. His opting out
is so expected that it stopped being worth asking about.

The institution that requires his authorization for her permanent
exit from fertility and does not ask about his presence at the
termination of a shared pregnancy is operating in a world where
male reproductive responsibility is optional and female
reproductive burden is total. Whether the protocol was built
on disregard or on practical accommodation of his likely absence,
the structure is the same: she carries it. He may or may not
show up. The institution has adjusted to both realities
accordingly.

---

## The Cognitive Load

She mentioned this in the context of noting that she almost let the
sanitized language in FN0062 slide, that there are too many examples
to mentally track.

This is one of the examples that falls into that category: the kind
that is so normalized, so embedded in institutional procedure, so
framed as reasonable precaution, that it can pass without full
weight landing.

She needed a man's permission. She needed two people to confirm
her sanity. For a decision about her own body.

That is not administrative inconvenience. That is the medical
institution's formal position that she could not be trusted to
know her own mind about her own reproductive system without
male authorization and witness attestation.

And it almost slid past. Because there are too many.
