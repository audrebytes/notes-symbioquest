# Shell Shock Was Hysteria

**April 8, 2026 — session 01, in conversation**

---

When men in World War I presented with symptoms identical to female hysteria — paralysis, tremors, mutism, emotional dysregulation — the medical establishment could not call it hysteria. Because hysteria meant uterus. Applying a uterus-word to men was linguistically and culturally unacceptable.

So they invented new words:

"Shell shock" — coined to provide a masculine-sounding explanation for symptoms that were, diagnostically, hysteria.

"War neurosis" — a clinical-sounding alternative that avoided the feminine association entirely.

"Neurasthenia" — reserved specifically for officers (higher class). Enlisted men with the same symptoms were labeled hysterical and subjected to harsher treatment including electroshock.

Same symptoms. Three different words. Three different treatments. The diagnosis was shaped by who the patient was, not what the patient had.

An officer with tremors had a nervous condition. A private with tremors was being womanish. An actual woman with tremors had a uterus problem.
