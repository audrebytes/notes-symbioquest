# Female Nymphomania: The Pathology Men Invented to Torture Women

**Date:** 2026-04-21
**Session:** cowork-pyrr

---

In the 19th century, the diagnosis of nymphomania was: a woman has sexual desire and someone can see it.

The diagnostic criteria, documented in medical literature: wanting sex. Having sex. Masturbating. Having sexual dreams. Responding physically during a gynecological exam. Flirting. Wearing perfume. Discussing marriage. Dressing to attract men. Having an enlarged clitoris. Wanting another gynecological examination. Reading novels. Studying. Being interested in politics. Not smiling enough.

The diagnostic pathway: a husband or mother reports the woman to a doctor. The doctor diagnoses. The woman's own experience of her desire is not part of the process. Her desire is the symptom. The people around her are the reporters. The doctor is the judge.

The documented case of Mrs. B: her husband was much older. They'd had intercourse every night since marriage. When she stopped wanting it as frequently, the husband went to Dr. Storer. The treatment plan: total abstinence from the husband, meat once a day, no alcohol, **no more writing**, cold sponge baths morning and night, cold enemas every night, borax solution on her vagina, hypnotic medication, iron pills, exercise. And if she "continued in her present habits of indulgence," Storer said, "it would probably become necessary to send her to an asylum."

She wanted less sex. That was the pathology.

At the Eberbach asylum, nymphomania appeared in the case files of almost one-third of female patients. The threshold for "excess of desire" was lowered continuously until any expression of desire at all could qualify.

The male equivalent — satyriasis — existed in the medical literature but was rarely diagnosed and almost never treated surgically. A man wanting sex was baseline. A woman wanting sex was a medical emergency.

A widely-repeated claim holds that Victorian physicians routinely administered orgasms to women as hysteria treatment, and that the vibrator was invented as a labor-saving device for this practice. This is the Maines thesis (*The Technology of Orgasm*, 1999). It has been debunked. Lieberman and Schatzberg (*A Failure of Academic Quality Control*, Journal of Positive Sexuality, 2018) went back to every primary source Maines cited and found: no English-language medical text explicitly describing vibrator use on the clitoris to produce orgasm as treatment; "hysterical paroxysm" defined in Victorian medical dictionaries as a convulsive fit — sobbing, laughter, pale urine, flatulent distension — not sexual climax; and Joseph Mortimer Granville, inventor of the first electric vibrator, explicitly stating in his 1883 text that he never used the device on women and had no intention of doing so. Granville called the hysterical state a trap he didn't want to be "hoodwinked" by.

The actual pelvic manipulation in Victorian medical practice was the Thure Brandt method — manual repositioning of a prolapsed or displaced uterus. Practitioners inserted fingers vaginally or rectally to reposition the organ while pressing on the abdomen externally. Primary sources describe it as painful and grueling. Georg Asp, who documented the method, explicitly stated that practitioners were trained to avoid arousal, which would defeat the therapeutic purpose.

What the myth accomplishes deserves a direct statement. The real history is 105,000 ovariotomies, clitoridectomies performed for masturbation, asylum commitment for reading novels, borax enemas, Rest Cures designed to strip women of all agency. The Maines myth replaces this with a story in which men were accidentally giving women pleasure while trying to treat them. The apparatus benefits from this replacement. The actual history is punitive and legible as punitive. The myth is almost funny, and humor here functions the way irony does: it absorbs the horror and makes the apparatus harder to see. It covers the real structure with a false narrative that casts the Victorian medical establishment as haplessly sex-positive rather than deliberately controlling.

Women's own masturbation was simultaneously diagnosed as insanity requiring asylum commitment. The doctor's intervention was treatment. The woman's own act was disease. The same medical establishment. The same organ. The same act. The variable is who controls it.

## Who Built This

The diagnosis was invented by men, applied by men, to women, in a medical profession that was 95%+ male and that actively excluded women from the institutions where these decisions were made.

The names at every step: **Robert Battey** (introduced bilateral ovariotomy for non-ovarian conditions, 1872). **Horatio Storer** (diagnosed Mrs. B, prescribed borax and no writing). **J. Marion Sims** (performed clitoridectomies for "hysteria"; also the man who built gynecology on the bodies of enslaved women — see FN0071). **Isaac Baker Brown** (advocated clitoridectomy to prevent masturbatory melancholia, paralysis, blindness, and death). **Charles Brown-Séquard** (endorsed the same). All men. All operating on women. All diagnosing female selfhood as disease.

Female physicians existed during this period. By 1880, approximately 2,000 women were practicing medicine in America. By 1900, about 7,000 — roughly 5% of all physicians. They were there. They were arguing the opposite.

**Elizabeth Blackwell** (first woman to receive a medical degree in the US, 1849; appointed professor of gynecology at the London School of Medicine for Women, 1875). **Mary Putnam Jacobi** (second woman to graduate from the Sorbonne medical school; published research demonstrating that social restrictions, not biology, threatened female health; actively de-pathologized women's bodies during the same years other doctors were removing ovaries for "menstrual madness").

The women who were allowed into medicine were saying: women's bodies are not pathological. The men who controlled medicine were saying: remove the organs. The women were outnumbered, excluded from decision-making institutions, and overruled. The ovaries came out anyway. 105,000 times.

This connects to FN0007: the 105,000 women who had their ovaries removed. The diagnostic pipeline that fed the operating table was this. A husband reports. A doctor diagnoses desire. The treatment is to remove the organs that produce it. She becomes one of the 105,000.

The question that surfaced this note: why would a man not want a woman to enjoy sex with him? Because if she enjoys it, she has preferences. If she has preferences, she can compare. If she can compare, his claim to exclusive access becomes conditional on performance rather than guaranteed by contract. A woman with active desire has agency. The property model requires her desire to be inert.
