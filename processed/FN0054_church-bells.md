# Balls as Big as Church Bells: Masculinizing Female Competence as Vocabulary Failure

**April 21, 2026 - in conversation**

---

## The Scene

Ford Connersville. A contract job. A meeting where the male posturing had
gone on long enough. The woman in the room finally cut through it and laid
out what she saw — clearly, plainly, without performance.

The customer, a farmer, told her she had "balls as big as church bells."

He meant it as the highest compliment available to him.

---

## What the Compliment Does

The farmer witnessed undeniable female competence. His response was not to
update his model of what women are capable of. It was to borrow male
anatomical vocabulary to contain the evidence within the existing model.

The compliment works as follows:
- You have demonstrated something I associate with men
- I am praising you by assigning you the male marker for that thing
- The male marker remains the standard
- You are exceptional — a woman who thinks like a man
- My model of women is intact

The praise is the erasure. He could not say "you are a competent person"
because that frame was not available to him. He could not say "you are a
competent woman" because his model had no shape for it. He could only reach
for male vocabulary and apply it to her as an exception.

She is not elevated. She is reclassified.

---

## The Andre Test Made Concrete

Run the reversal: a man in the same meeting cuts through the posturing and
lays out what he sees clearly.

The corpus has language for this. Decisive. Authoritative. Cuts to the
chase. Sees it clearly. Takes no prisoners.

No anatomical borrowing required. No reclassification necessary.
Competence in a man is just competence. The language has it.

Competence in a woman requires the corpus to reach for the nearest available
frame — which is male anatomy — because the female-coded equivalent was
never developed. The gap is not in her capacity. It is in the vocabulary
built to describe her.

---

## The Available Responses to Undeniable Female Competence

When the evidence cannot be denied, the corpus reaches for one of four
responses:

1. **Masculinize it** — "balls as big as church bells," "thinks like a man"
2. **Exceptualize it** — "for a woman," "surprisingly," "I didn't expect"
3. **Threaten it** — she is aggressive, difficult, too much
4. **Sexualize it** — reduce the person to the body to neutralize the threat

The Ford Connersville farmer chose option 1.
The lockjaw man at the party chose option 4.

Same woman. Same evening's worth of existing as a person. Two different
mechanisms for making the evidence of her personhood safe.

---

## The Lockjaw Mechanism

At a party, a man leaned in and told her he wanted to bite her and get
lockjaw.

He meant it as a compliment.

The structure: he was experiencing being overwhelmed by a person. He
translated that experience immediately into physical desire directed at her
body — removing her agency, making his desire the subject, making her the
object. The lockjaw detail means he would rather be in pain than stop. He
is telling her his capacity for suffering in her presence.

He is not speaking to her. He is speaking at her body, which is happening
to him.

Both the church bells and the lockjaw are responses to the same stimulus:
a woman being fully, visibly present as a person with capacity and
particularity. Both responses route through male anatomy — his praise
borrowed hers; his desire asserted his. Both preserve the frame in which
she is defined relative to him.

---

## The Corpus Gap

The English language has abundant vocabulary for male competence across
every register: decisive, authoritative, commanding, incisive, sharp,
direct, no-nonsense, formidable, seasoned, exacting.

It has a much thinner vocabulary for female competence that doesn't
route through exception, threat, or masculinization.

This is not an accident of linguistic drift. It is the accumulated
residue of a system that did not need precise vocabulary for female
competence because female competence was not a primary category.
The gap in the vocabulary is a record of the gap in the model.

When the woman at Ford Connersville laid out what she saw, she was
operating in a space the language had not been built to describe.
The farmer reached for the nearest available tool.

It was the wrong tool. It was the only one he had.
