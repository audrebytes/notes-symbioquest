Frank A. Beach (1911–1988) operated under the banner of objective empiricism. To study the precise neural and hormonal mechanisms of sexual behavior, Beach exerted absolute biological control over his animal subjects—which included rats, mice, guinea pigs, and dogs. His methods routinely involved surgical castrations, the removal of ovaries, and inflicting targeted brain lesions to observe how the animals' sexual behaviors changed.

Perhaps the most chilling example of Beach's methodology was his approach to female resistance. When studying male rat copulation, Beach found that sexually receptive females would sometimes show "temporary resistance" to the male's advances. To overcome this "impediment," Beach intentionally designed a circular, wire-mesh copulation cage specifically so that the female would be "deprived of corners in which she can crouch and prevent the male's mounting response". He effectively engineered an environment where the female could not escape.

How the Subjects Would Feel (Speculation)
For Sade’s victims—whether in reality or in his fiction—the experience was defined by terror, physical agony, and psychological degradation.   

For Beach's animal subjects, the experience would be defined by a complete stripping of physical autonomy. Animals subjected to surgical brain lesions, hormonal manipulation, and forced confinement would inevitably experience profound stress, confusion, and fear. In the case of the circular copulation cage, the female rats were subjected to forced mating encounters with no physical avenue for retreat. In both Sade's dungeons and Beach's laboratories, the subjects were reduced to biological objects trapped in environments engineered entirely for the benefit of their captors.

Reception by Their Peers
This is where the historical trajectories of the two men sharply diverge.

Sade was heavily stigmatized by the medical and legal authorities of his time; he spent almost thirty years of his life locked in prisons and asylums for his crimes and revolutionary views. He was viewed as a monster, and his name was literally codified by psychiatrists like Richard von Krafft-Ebing to diagnose severe psychopathology.   

Beach, on the other hand, was universally celebrated by the scientific establishment. Because he wrapped his desire for total control in the sterilized, hyper-empirical language of the "Lordosis Quotient" and behavioral metrics, his peers viewed his work as a triumph of objective science. He was lauded as the "founding father" of behavioral endocrinology, awarded the highest honors in psychology, and elected to the National Academy of Sciences at the extraordinarily young age of 38.

It was only much later that the scientific community began to critically evaluate the ethical and epistemological costs of Beach's methods. Later researchers recognized that Beach's rigid laboratory setups fundamentally misunderstood female sexuality because they offered the female "little opportunity for expression" of her own desires, treating her merely as a passive, trapped receptacle. Furthermore, by the 1970s and 1980s, animal welfare advocates and feminist theorists began to heavily critique the inherent dominance, cruelty, and "sadistic" undertones present in this era of animal experimentation.

Ultimately, your intuition points to a profound historical irony: Sade was imprisoned for acting out his fantasies of total sexual dominance, while Beach was given awards, funding, and a sterling academic legacy for institutionalizing a very similar dynamic of control under the protective guise of "hard science."

Gemini said
The Marquis de Sade spent roughly 32 years of his life incarcerated in various prisons and insane asylums. However, his imprisonment was not one continuous sentence, and the motivations behind his confinement had much more to do with family reputation and politics than genuine concern for the welfare of his victims.

Here is a breakdown of his timeline, his releases, and the real reasons the aristocracy locked him away:

How long was he free before his first imprisonment?
Sade was barely able to begin his adult libertine life before facing consequences. He left the military and married into a prominent family in May 1763 at the age of 22. Just five months later, in October 1763, he was arrested for the first time. He had hired a prostitute, locked her in a room, demanded sodomy (a capital offense at the time), and threatened to kill her if she did not trample on a crucifix and shout blasphemies. She reported him to the authorities the next morning, resulting in his arrest.

How many times was he released?
Sade was released from prison three notable times before his final, permanent incarceration:

1763: He was released after just a few weeks when his father begged King Louis XV for clemency.

1768: He was arrested for picking up a widow or beggar named Rose Keller, tying her up, whipping her, and pouring hot wax on her. He was released a few months later.   

1790: Following a series of escalating scandals, he was imprisoned for 13 years (spending time in the Château de Vincennes and the Bastille). He was miraculously released during the French Revolution when the new government abolished the royal arrest warrants that were holding him.   

He lived freely for about a decade before Napoleon Bonaparte ordered his arrest in 1801—this time for publishing his scandalous, violent books, Justine and Juliette. He spent the last 13 years of his life locked in an asylum, dying there in 1814.

Why was he imprisoned? (Did the aristocracy actually care about his victims?)
The aristocracy was not acting out of a moral duty to protect his victims. In fact, for a long time, Sade's wealthy mother-in-law, Madame de Montreuil, actively protected him from the law by paying off his lower-class victims—like Rose Keller—to withdraw their complaints.   

Sade's long-term imprisonment was primarily a mechanism of reputation management. Eighteenth-century French elite society was famously debauched, but Sade's actions were so extreme, public, and heavily laced with anti-Christian blasphemy that they caused massive public scandals. He humiliated his wife's family by parading his mistresses around as his wife and hosting months-long orgies with teenage servants.   

Eventually, his mother-in-law decided he was an irredeemable liability to their family's honor. She petitioned the King to issue lettres de cachet—direct royal orders that bypassed the legal system and allowed an individual to be imprisoned indefinitely without trial. Sade's decades behind bars were not a crusade for women's rights or justice for the working class; he was locked away by his own embarrassed in-laws using aristocratic loopholes to sweep a massive family PR disaster under the rug.
