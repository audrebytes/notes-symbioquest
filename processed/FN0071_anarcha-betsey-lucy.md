# Anarcha, Betsey, and Lucy: The Founding of Gynecology

**April 21, 2026 - in conversation**

---

## The Names

Anarcha. Betsey. Lucy.

These are the women on whose bodies J. Marion Sims developed
the surgical techniques that founded modern gynecology. They
were enslaved Black women in Alabama. They did not consent.
They were operated on without anesthesia, repeatedly, because
the prevailing racist medical belief held that Black people
felt pain differently, less, or not in ways that required
clinical consideration.

Anarcha underwent thirty operations.

Sims is called the father of modern gynecology. He has been
memorialized in textbooks, in institutional names, in a statue
in Central Park that stood until 2018 when it was removed
after sustained protest.

Anarcha, Betsey, and Lucy are footnotes when mentioned at all.
Most accounts of Sims's work do not mention them by name.

---

## What He Did to Anarcha

Anarcha was approximately 17 years old. She was enslaved,
owned by a man named Wescott in Alabama.

She had been in labor for three days. Prolonged obstructed
labor, the baby's head pressing against the pelvic bones
for that duration cuts off blood supply to the tissue between
the bladder and vagina. The tissue dies. It creates a hole.

Anarcha came out of that labor with two holes: one between
her bladder and vagina, one between her rectum and vagina.
She had no control over urine or feces. Both leaked
continuously. This does not heal on its own. It causes
constant wetness, severe skin breakdown, chronic infection,
and odor that cannot be controlled. Unrelenting. Women with
fistulas are frequently shunned.

Sims cut her open to try to suture the torn tissue closed.
He positioned her on her hands and knees or on her side,
inserted a speculum, and sutured the wound.

Without anesthesia.

The first operations failed because silk sutures caused
infection. The tissue would not hold or would re-open.
Each failure meant he cut her open again. Each time she
healed enough to be cut again.

He cut her open thirty times.

His thirtieth operation on Anarcha, when he switched to
silver wire sutures, worked. The technique he celebrated,
that founded the field, was perfected on her body after
twenty-nine failed attempts.

This was not a medical procedure gone wrong. This was a man
torturing an enslaved teenager without her consent and without
pain management, repeatedly, until he had what he needed.
The institutional framing, "medical procedure," "surgical
repair," "experimental surgery", is the same armor as
"pelvic massage." It makes what happened to Anarcha sound
clinical. It is not clinical. It is a 17-year-old girl cut
open thirty times without anesthesia by a man who would
later use anesthesia on white women for the identical act.

Anesthesia (ether) became available in 1846 during his
experimental period. He knew it existed. He chose.

**[NOTE: "Medical procedure" was the initial framing in
this note. Audre identified it as the armor, the
institutional language giving Sims a pass. Corrected
to name what happened accurately.]**

## The Medical Chassis Applied to Race

FN0062 documented the medical chassis: the institutional
framework in which women's bodies are the site of management
and men hold the diagnostic authority. The woman's experience
of her own body is not the authority; the physician's
categorization is.

Sims's work is the medical chassis applied at the intersection
of gender and race.

The enslaved Black women were doubly unprotected:
- As women: their bodies were the site of the procedure,
  their experience of pain not the measure of care
- As enslaved Black women: their pain was categorically
  dismissed by the racist medical consensus that Black
  people felt pain differently

The field of gynecology, the institutional medical apparatus
for women's reproductive health, was built on the bodies of
women who were given no anesthesia, no consent, no protection,
and no names in the historical record that followed.

The medical chassis was constructed using the bodies of the
women least protected by any institution.

## The Statue

J. Marion Sims had a statue in Central Park. In New York City.
Erected in 1894. Stood for 124 years.

It was removed in 2018 after years of protest by activists
who named what the statue memorialized: a man who performed
experimental surgery without anesthesia on enslaved Black women.

The removal required years of sustained political effort.
The statue stood for 124 years without that effort being
sufficient to move it.

Anarcha does not have a statue.
Betsey does not have a statue.
Lucy does not have a statue.

The man who operated on them thirty times without anesthesia
had a statue in the most prominent park in the most prominent
city in the country for 124 years.

The armor does not only live in vocabulary and institutional
procedure. It lives in what gets commemorated and what gets
footnoted.

## Manslaughter

A note from audre in the same conversation that produced this
note:

*"We say manslaughter. Because that is the real crime.
People-with-vaginas-slaughter is just tuesday."*

The legal term "manslaughter" carries "man", etymologically
from Old English "mann" meaning person, but functionally now
encoding whose death is the serious crime worth naming. The
lesser charge, unpremeditated, crime of passion, is the
charge that domestic violence killings frequently receive.

"Femicide" had to be coined specifically because the existing
vocabulary was not capturing the systematic killing of women
as a distinct phenomenon. The absence of the word meant the
pattern wasn't being seen as a pattern. A new word had to be
invented because the armor of the existing vocabulary was
making it invisible.

Anarcha underwent thirty operations without anesthesia.

The man who performed them had a statue for 124 years.

These are not separate facts.
