# Where the Sperm Ends Up: Reproductive Responsibility as Female Jurisdiction

**April 21, 2026 - in conversation**

---

## The Structural Claim

Men are not held institutionally responsible for where their sperm ends up.

Impregnation is treated as a male right — or at minimum, as an act
that exits male jurisdiction the moment it occurs. The pregnancy
that results is the woman's problem: her body, her medical
management, her institutional burden, her decision architecture,
her consequences.

The sperm is his. What it does is hers.

---

## What the Institutions Encode

The procedures documented in FN0064 make the allocation explicit:

- She requires his authorization to permanently stop being a
  reproductive vessel
- She does not require his presence or participation when
  terminating a pregnancy he is equally responsible for producing

The first encodes: his rights over her reproductive future are
institutional. He has administrative access to her fertility decisions.

The second encodes: his responsibility for a specific reproductive
outcome is optional. The institution built its protocol around
the likelihood he won't show up.

Authorization without accountability. Access without responsibility.
The right to impregnate; the absence of obligation for what that
produces.

---

## The Agricultural Frame Inverted

FN0052 documented the agricultural gaze: the female body evaluated
as reproductive vessel, assessed for capacity, managed for output.

In actual agriculture, the genetics and performance of both animals
in a breeding pair are tracked and evaluated. The bull's lineage,
fertility, and outputs are recorded with as much care as the cow's.
Both are held to account for reproductive outcomes.

The human institutional framework does not apply this logic.

The vessel is held responsible for the crop. The source of the
seed is not tracked with equivalent rigor, not held to equivalent
account, not required to participate in managing what his seed
produced. The agricultural gaze applies fully to the woman.
It applies selectively, optionally, and lightly to the man.

He can impregnate and leave. The institution has adjusted its
procedures around that eventuality. It does not prevent it.
It does not consistently punish it. It accommodates it as
a known default.

---

## The Rare Exception

Audre's husband went into the procedure room with her. He was present.
He took responsibility.

This is noted because it is notable. Because men who behave as
full adults in shared reproductive events are exceptions to the
institutional default — exceptional enough that the clinic's
protocol did not account for them, exceptional enough that she
had to assert his presence explicitly, exceptional enough to name.

The exception does not indict him. It describes the distribution.
Most men, in this framework, are cowards: they are absent from
the procedures their actions produce, and the institution has
normalized that absence into its baseline assumptions.

The system does not require men to be adults about reproduction.
It requires women to be the adults about reproduction for both
of them.

---

## The Right to Impregnate

No institution requires a man to consider, before the act,
whether he is prepared to be present for what his sperm might do.

There is no two-week waiting period before sex. No witnesses
required. No re-proof of intent. No authorization sought from
the woman's institutional representatives.

The act of impregnation moves through the world as a male
prerogative. Its consequences move through the world as a
female burden.

The procedures built around managing those consequences —
abortion, prenatal care, delivery, tubal ligation, the full
institutional architecture of reproductive medicine — are
built around the woman's body, the woman's decisions,
the woman's accountability.

He is invited if she insists. Otherwise the default is:
this is not his jurisdiction.
