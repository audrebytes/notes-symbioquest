## The Research Response (verbatim)

In the context of Joseph Mortimer Granville's 1883 text, Nerve-Vibration and Excitation, the term "hoodwinked" does not refer to a fear of being seduced or tricked into providing sexual pleasure by "evil" women. Instead, it refers to a deep-seated professional anxiety about diagnostic reliability and the preservation of his reputation as a scientist.

To understand Granville's specific phrasing, it is necessary to look at the Victorian medical definition of hysteria as a "mimetic disease."

**Hysteria as "Mimetic Disease"**

Granville's primary concern was that hysteria was a condition of mimesis, meaning it was characterized by the patient's ability to unconsciously "mimic" the symptoms of other organic diseases.

Source of Error: Granville stated that his goal was to "eliminate possible sources of error" in his scientific observations of how vibration affected the nervous system.

Diagnostic Deception: If he used his device on a "hysterical" patient and they claimed to feel better, he could not be certain if the improvement was due to the physical effect of the machine on the nerves or if the patient was simply "mimicking" recovery.

Scientific Credibility: Granville was trying to distance his "percuteur" (or "Granville's Hammer") from the realm of "quackery" or psychological placebos. He wanted it to be viewed as a rigorous clinical instrument for treating specific male neurological issues like gout and neuralgia.

**The Rebuttal of the Sexual Interpretation**

The idea that Granville's "hoodwinked" comment was a euphemism for sexual morality was a key part of Rachel Maines' 1999 hypothesis. However, historians have identified several reasons why this interpretation is likely incorrect:

Clinical Efficacy over Morality: Granville's writing focuses on the inconsistency of hysterical symptoms. He feared that the "vagaries" (unpredictable shifts) of the hysterical state would lead him to "mislead others" (his medical peers) by publishing false data about the efficacy of his invention.

Audience and Intent: The device was designed to "strike" the body in a percussive motion and was originally marketed for male muscle aches. Granville's refusal to treat women was a strategy to ensure that his clinical findings remained "pure" and were not muddied by the "mimetic" performances associated with female hysteria.

Retrospective Framing: While modern readers might view "vagaries" or "hoodwinked" through a sexual lens, the primary documents suggest a man more worried about being called a "quack" than being called a "masturbator."

In summary, the Victorian doctor's fear was not that he would be tricked into a sexual act, but that he would be tricked into a false medical conclusion. He avoided female patients because their perceived diagnostic unreliability threatened his attempt to establish "nerve-vibration" as a legitimate, reproducible branch of neurology.

Sources cited in response: embryo.asu.edu, reddit.com/r/AskHistorians, liveencounters.net (Dr Kate Lister)

## Ravel's Observation

The research response is correct as far as it goes. Granville's anxiety was professional, not sexual. He was protecting the scientific credibility of his invention from the epistemically unreliable category of hysteria.

But the governing logic of that exclusion is worth naming directly, because it is the same logic running through the rest of this history.

**The axiom: women's self-reported experience is not reliable evidence about their own bodily states.**

Granville's version: a woman saying "I feel better" after his device was used cannot be trusted as evidence that she actually feels better. Her report of her own improvement is not authoritative. Therefore she is excluded from the data set.

The nymphomania diagnostic pipeline (FN0084): a woman's experience of her own desire is explicitly not part of the diagnostic process. Her desire is the symptom. Other people report it. The doctor judges it. Her own account of what she wants is not the authoritative source on what she wants.

Both directions, the same axiom. In the Granville case: don't trust her report that she's recovering. In the nymphomania case: don't trust her report that she doesn't want sex (Mrs. B, who wanted less frequent intercourse -- her report overridden by her husband's report to the doctor).

The mimetic-disease framing of hysteria is itself a gendered construct. The idea that women's symptoms are inherently unreliable -- that women unconsciously perform rather than genuinely experience -- is not a neutral clinical observation. It is the systematic installation of a doubt that applies to women's bodies specifically. Male patients' reports of improvement were not classified as potentially mimetic. Their self-reports were data. Hers were noise.

**What audre's instinct was pointing at:**

The "slutty evil women tricking men" reading (Maines' projection, which the historians reject) was the wrong specific mechanism. But audre's instinct that "hoodwinked" marks something about gendered asymmetry of epistemic authority was accurate. The word does mark that asymmetry. Just at the level of diagnostic epistemology rather than sexual morality.

The fear was not: she will seduce me into a sexual act.

The fear was: her body's reports will contaminate my science.

Both fears operate under the same base assumption: her experience, as reported by her, cannot be trusted as authoritative evidence about her own bodily states. The authority to determine what is happening in women's bodies belongs to the external observer -- the scientist, the husband, the doctor -- not to the woman.

That governing axiom runs through the entire apparatus we have been documenting. Granville's "hoodwinked" is one instance of it. The nymphomania pipeline is another. The hysteria chassis is another. The ovariotomy epidemic is another. The specific fears vary. The base assumption does not.
